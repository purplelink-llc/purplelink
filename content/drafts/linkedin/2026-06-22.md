## Option 1

I spent part of this week designing the verdict taxonomy for a new feature in Paper Review: a citation support audit that checks whether the sources cited in a paper actually back the claims attached to them.

The tricky part was naming the verdicts. The system fetches the abstract of each cited paper from OpenAlex or Semantic Scholar, then assesses whether the abstract supports the specific sentence that cites it. Abstracts do not contain everything in a paper. A source might not support a claim at the abstract level while fully supporting it in the methods section.

So "not supported" is honest but incomplete. What I landed on was five verdicts: Supported, Partially supported, Not supported by abstract, Contradicted, and Source unavailable. The key phrase is "by abstract." The report says "verify against the full text" rather than "this claim is wrong." The audit can surface a Contradicted finding, but even then the report quotes both the specific abstract sentence and the specific claim so the author reads the evidence directly and decides.

The reason to be careful about framing is that the feature is auditing someone's research paper. Framing findings accusatorially is a worse outcome than being conservative. The tool surfaces evidence. The author decides what to do with it.

If you have built or used automated review tools, I would be curious how you handled the problem of surfacing findings without overclaiming them.

## Option 2

I finished building a daily newsletter this week that I set up for myself. Every morning at 6am ET, a Modal cron job wakes up, fetches from 35 sources, passes the results through a Claude curation step, writes a static HTML post to my blog, and sends a Buttondown email.

The 35 sources span academic preprints (arXiv, Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex, HuggingFace Daily Papers), AI blogs, cybersecurity feeds (Krebs on Security, Schneier on Security, Bleeping Computer, CISA KEV), and a few finance and startup newsletters. Source failures degrade silently: if an individual feed is down, the run continues with whatever came back from the rest.

The curation step is one Claude call per run. It gets a profile describing my research interests, a dump of all raw items, and returns structured JSON with scores, category assignments, and short editorial notes on each selected item. The cap is 23 items per digest across six sections. I built the abort condition too: if fewer than five items survive curation, nothing publishes that day.

I built this for myself. It runs whether or not I check it, and the posts go to a public blog page so anyone can subscribe. The architecture is Modal for compute, GitHub API writes for publishing, and Netlify auto-deploys on push.

If you have a reading list problem across a few disparate areas, I would be interested in how you currently handle it.

## Option 3

The starting point for Paper Review's new citation support audit was a failure mode I kept seeing in peer review: a paper makes a strong empirical claim in the results section, cites two or three sources, and when you look up those sources, the claim is not there. Not contradicted. Just absent.

Most citation checkers verify that the reference exists and that the title matches what you typed. That is useful, but it does not tell you whether the cited paper says what you claim it says.

The new feature extracts claim-citation pairs from the paper body, fetches the abstract for each cited source from OpenAlex and Semantic Scholar, and runs them through an LLM assessment. Each pair gets a verdict: Supported, Partially supported, Not supported by abstract, Contradicted, or Source unavailable. Claims are ranked by salience first, with a hard cap of 40 assessments per run, so the audit focuses on the load-bearing citations rather than the background references.

The report quotes both sides: the sentence from the paper and the sentence from the abstract that bears on it. For non-Supported verdicts, the report notes explicitly that the abstract may not capture the full-text argument. The author reads evidence, not conclusions.

This runs inside the existing Paper Review pipeline with no extra upload or step.

If you review papers regularly and have encountered mismatched citations, I would be interested in which fields or paper types you see it most often in.

## Selected

Option 3 -- it addresses the most recognizable pain point for the target audience, gives the most specific and honest account of how the feature works, and the engagement question is concrete enough to generate real replies.
