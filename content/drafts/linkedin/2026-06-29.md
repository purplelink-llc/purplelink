## Option 1

Paper Review deletes your manuscript from Purplelink's servers the moment the report renders in your browser. Not archived. Not retained for analytics. Gone. The only copy is the one you download or copy before closing the tab.

I want to explain why I built it this way, because it affects how the tool can be used.

Researchers upload unpublished work. A manuscript before submission often represents months or years of unreleased findings. Holding that data after the review is complete means holding something I have no reason to hold. It also means that a data breach or a third-party legal request could expose work that was never intended to be public.

The tradeoff is that customer support gets harder. If someone loses the report by closing the tab, I cannot restore it. I handle that by offering a refund rather than a retry. The guide is explicit: download or copy the Markdown report before closing the tab.

The manuscript is sent to Anthropic's Claude API, which retains inputs for up to 30 days for abuse monitoring. That is a real caveat, and it is disclosed in the guide. For papers containing identifiable patient data, the guide recommends redacting before uploading.

Ephemeral-by-default is not a novel privacy model, but it is a deliberate one. Most automated review tools do not explain what happens to a manuscript after the review runs.

If you have thought about data handling when uploading unpublished work to a web tool, I would be curious what your expectation is.

## Option 2

Paper Review's Standard tier runs four reviewer personas in parallel: a Methodology Critic, a Statistical Skeptic, a Data Integrity Officer, and an Editor-in-Chief. Each red-teams the manuscript independently. A consensus filter surfaces findings that more than one persona raised, on the logic that if two reviewers caught the same problem, it is not an artifact of one model's priors.

The Deep Review tier adds a second pass. In the first pass, each persona reviews the manuscript on its own. In the second pass, each persona gets all four first-round reports and revisits its findings with that context. The second pass is where borderline findings get tested and inter-persona disagreements get resolved.

A methodology problem that the Methodology Critic rated as borderline reads differently to the Statistical Skeptic once the Skeptic can see that the Critic had the same doubt. The personas can argue rather than just summarize.

I built the two-pass approach as a separate tier at $8 rather than making it the default because it costs more compute and takes longer. Standard at $3 catches the obvious problems. Deep Review is for the round before a high-rejection-rate venue or a resubmission after major reviewer comments. Standard runs in 4-8 minutes. Deep Review typically takes 12-15 minutes for a 20-page manuscript.

If you have been through peer review at a high-bar journal, I would be curious what the reviewers caught that you had missed in your own read.

## Option 3

This week I fixed a quality problem in the Purplelink Daily Digest curation. The root cause was straightforward: I was passing too many raw items to the LLM in a single call.

The harvest step pulls from 35+ sources and returns 600-800 items on a typical day. Passing all of them to Claude in one call caused context saturation. The model's attention degrades across very long inputs, and it started favoring items near the top of the list rather than the best items across the full set.

The fix was a prefilter: before the curation call, sort each category by freshness and a rough relevance score, then cap input at 18 items per category. That brings the input from roughly 750 items down to around 100 before any LLM work happens. Claude then selects from a manageable candidate pool and applies the editorial rules.

The final digest still caps at 23 items across six sections, so nothing changed for readers. The 23 items are drawn from better candidates now, rather than whatever survived a 750-item context.

I also tightened the curator's voice rules during this pass: third person throughout, short sentences, and an explicit note when two items connect to the same threat actor or event.

If you have built something that uses LLMs for content curation at volume, I would be curious how you handled context saturation.

## Selected

Option 2 -- it is the most concrete and specific, addresses a recognizable pain point for the target audience (researchers who have faced tough peer review), and explains a real product design decision honestly rather than abstractly.
