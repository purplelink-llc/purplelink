## Option 1

When you copy text from a research PDF and paste it somewhere, equations usually come through garbled. A formula that reads clearly in the paper becomes a string of symbols that means nothing to a language model or to a search index.

The PDF format stores math as positioned glyphs, not as logical expressions. A summation sign is a font character. The exponent is another character placed nearby at a specific coordinate. There is no semantic structure encoding "this is a formula." When an extractor reads the file, it sees characters, not math.

I published a blog post this week on this problem: getting a paper into an LLM without losing the equations. The PDF to Structured Data tool at purplelink.llc/tools/pdf-structure/ addresses it by detecting math regions and emitting them as LaTeX strings. So a sum across n terms comes through as $$\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i$$ rather than as scrambled Unicode. That LaTeX is what you paste into a prompt when you want a model to explain a derivation or verify a step.

The blog post at purplelink.llc/blog/pdf-to-llm-without-losing-the-math/ walks through a realistic session with a methodology section that has equations. The tool is free, no account needed, files processed in memory.

If you have been losing math content on the way out of a PDF, I would be curious which papers were giving you trouble.

## Option 2

I published two pieces about the PDF to Structured Data tool this week: a blog post on feeding research papers into LLMs without losing the math, and a step-by-step guide for converting a paper PDF to clean Markdown.

The guide came from a specific gap I noticed: the tool outputs both Markdown and JSON, and the choice between them matters depending on what you are doing. Markdown is for immediate pasting into a chat assistant or a notes app. JSON is for when you need block-level metadata -- each element's type, page number, and bounding box -- for a retrieval pipeline or an indexing system. Most researchers arriving at the tool for the first time do not have that context, and a tooltip does not communicate it well enough.

So I wrote a guide that covers the six-step workflow from upload to paste-into-assistant, explains the difference between the two outputs, and answers three questions that came up in early testing: what happens with two-column layouts, when to reach for the JSON, and whether arXiv preprints work as well as published papers (they do).

The guide is at purplelink.llc/guides/research-paper-pdf-to-markdown/ and the tool is free at purplelink.llc/tools/pdf-structure/.

If you regularly bring papers into an AI assistant and have a step where the conversion breaks down, I would like to hear what that step is.

## Option 3

I have been running paper PDFs through the PDF to Structured Data tool this week while writing a blog post about it. The test case I kept returning to: a methods section with equations, a results section with a comparison table, and body text in a two-column layout.

Two things change when the output is clean. One is math. A derivation comes through as LaTeX, which a language model reads correctly rather than having to reconstruct from garbled characters. The other is tables. A results table with six columns is a real Markdown table in the output, not whitespace-separated rows that have to be parsed back into structure. In both cases you could feed the garbled content to the model and it might work through it, but you are spending tokens on parsing errors and introducing ambiguity that is not in the original paper.

The workflow is short: download a paper from arXiv or a journal site, drag it into purplelink.llc/tools/pdf-structure/, copy the Markdown, paste into whatever assistant you use. About fifteen seconds for most papers.

The blog post walking through a realistic session is at purplelink.llc/blog/pdf-to-llm-without-losing-the-math/. The tool is free and no account is needed.

If there is a step in your research workflow where the PDF-to-text conversion breaks down for you, I would be interested in which step that is.

## Selected

Option 3 -- it grounds the reader in a concrete test scenario, introduces a specific and honest observation (spending tokens on parsing errors) that the other options do not, and gives a workflow short enough to try immediately.
