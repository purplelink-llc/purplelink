## Option 1

Most word processors count all the text in a document. Journals that set word limits almost always mean main text only -- not references, not the appendix, not figure captions. Getting the right number has meant manual subtraction or running two separate word counts.

I published a guide this week on using Purplelink's Word Counter for this. The tool splits an uploaded manuscript into sections: Abstract, Methods, Results, Discussion, References, Appendix, Figure Captions. You toggle off the sections your journal excludes, and the count updates to show exactly what you are measuring.

The section detection has real limits. LaTeX files work cleanly because section commands are explicit. Word documents rely on heading styles. PDFs are the least reliable; a paper without consistent heading formatting may not split correctly. The guide notes this and suggests the workaround: export to Word or LaTeX, fix the headings, re-upload.

One decision I made deliberately: the tool does not try to auto-detect which sections a given journal excludes. That would require maintaining a journal database and would give researchers false confidence when it is incomplete or wrong. Explicit toggles leave the decision with the researcher, which is where it should be.

The guide is at purplelink.llc/guides/check-manuscript-word-limit/.

If you have found a reliable way to handle journal word limits as a researcher or editor, I would be curious what it is.

## Option 2

A common problem in academic papers: a citation appears in a methods or discussion section to support a specific claim, but when you pull the cited paper, it does not actually make that claim. The citation checks out in CrossRef. The paper exists. It just does not say what you say it says. This is hard to catch in self-review and usually only comes up when a peer reviewer does the work of looking.

Paper Review's citation audit runs two stages. First, it checks all references against CrossRef to confirm they exist and that author, title, and year match. Second, for the citations that carry the most methodological or empirical weight -- the ones supporting your central claims -- it fetches the source papers and reads them against what you have written. If the source does not hold up your claim, it flags the gap with a quoted passage from both.

This second stage is slower and more expensive than simple existence checking, so it does not run on every citation. The design assumption is that a wrong citation to a key claim matters more than a minor reference that is slightly off. The check runs deeper where the stakes are higher.

Each finding comes back quoted against the passage in your manuscript so you can verify or dismiss it yourself in under a minute.

If you review papers regularly, I would be curious whether you find citation mismatches increasing or whether they have always been common and are just easier to see now.

## Option 3

When I was designing Paper Review's output format, the obvious choice was a score. A number, maybe a grade letter. That is what most evaluation tools produce.

I built a checklist instead: findings ranked A, B, and C by severity, each one quoted against the specific passage in your manuscript it concerns. No overall score, no verdict.

The practical reason is that a score compresses everything into one number and loses the specifics. When you are 48 hours from a submission deadline, an 82 out of 100 tells you almost nothing useful. A list that says here are two things that need to be fixed, here are three to consider, and here are two that probably do not matter -- that you can work through.

The second reason is honesty. Paper Review makes mistakes. It reads documents with language models that occasionally misread context or flag something that is not actually a problem. A score would suggest more precision than is there. A checklist lets you verify each item in under a minute. You decide what to fix.

The checklist format also makes the tool's role clear. It is designed for the gap between "I think this is ready" and "I have confirmed I have not missed anything obvious" -- not a replacement for the judgment you have already brought to the paper.

If you have used manuscript feedback tools, I would be interested in what output format you found most useful for actually making changes.

## Selected

Option 2 -- it is the most mechanically specific, identifies a real and recognizable academic problem (citations that do not support their claims), explains the two-stage design clearly, and is honest about what the tool does not do.
