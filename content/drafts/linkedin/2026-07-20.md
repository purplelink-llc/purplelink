## Option 1

Double-blind submission has a specific failure mode that removing your name from the title page does not fix.

The body of a manuscript typically contains other identifying information: grant and contract numbers in the acknowledgements, IRB and ethics protocol IDs in the methods section, self-citation prose ("in our previous work [N]") where the cited entry's title includes your name, and named software or repositories you developed. A reviewer who searches any of those strings can identify the authors without the title page.

I shipped a guide this week on using Purplelink's Anonymity Check before submitting to a double-blind venue. The tool scans a PDF manuscript and flags seven categories of identifying information, quoting the exact text and location so you know what to fix. It costs $2 per scan.

The guide is honest about what the scan does not cover. Figures and supplementary files require a separate read-through. PDF metadata is out of scope entirely: a Word-generated PDF often carries the author's name in the Creator field, and clearing it is a separate step using your word processor's document-info panel.

The recommended workflow is: compile to PDF, scan, fix each finding in source, recompile, scan again to confirm.

Guide is at purplelink.llc/guides/anonymity-check-double-blind-manuscript/

If you have reviewed manuscripts at a double-blind venue, I would be curious which categories of identifying information you see most often.

## Option 2

Last Saturday I rewrote Purplelink's homepage. The previous version framed the site as a portfolio studio with a services grid and an operations registry. The primary call to action was "get in touch."

I rebuilt it around the products instead. The hero now has one call to action: join the ModernTex waitlist. Below that is a single proof line: the Scholar Utility Belt extension is used by 1,000 researchers and rated 4.5 stars on the Chrome Web Store. The services section and the contact form are gone.

The decision I spent the most time on was removing the contact form. It was prominently placed and I had used it to filter potential client work. I removed it because a contact form implies you take on work from whoever writes in. Purplelink is a product studio. That framing is wrong and it attracts the wrong kind of conversations.

The social proof line was the most straightforward improvement. A user count and a rating are verifiable and give a potential user something concrete to act on. The old hero copy had a line about "craft and intent" that said nothing comparable.

I also dropped "no shortcuts, no filler" from the hero. A statement about your own quality is harder to trust than demonstrated output. The proof line does that job more efficiently.

If you have rebuilt a product homepage, I am curious what you found easiest to cut.

## Option 3

Last week LinkedIn deprecated the API endpoint Purplelink uses to publish its daily research digest. The old /v2/ugcPosts path uses a nested content schema; the replacement, /rest/posts, uses a flat one, requires a versioned header computed at request time, and returns the new post ID in a response header rather than in the body.

Before deploying the migration, I ran a test against the live endpoint in DRAFT mode: a real authenticated request, with the new schema, to the production URL, but with visibility set to DRAFT so nothing published. The response came back as a 201 with the correct ID in x-restli-id. I deleted the test draft through the LinkedIn UI, then shipped.

The migration is 22 lines added and 17 removed in a single file. The safe-mode test took longer to plan than the code took to write, but it was the right call. The previous version of the publisher had a silent failure mode: local tests would pass, but nothing would appear on LinkedIn. That kind of failure compounds quietly before you notice it.

An automation that touches a third-party API and appears to succeed locally is not confirmed until you verify the output at the destination.

If you build automations that depend on third-party APIs, I am curious what your approach is for testing before shipping.

## Selected

Option 1 -- it is the most directly relevant to the academic researcher audience, explains a real and recognizable pre-submission problem most researchers have encountered, is honest about tool limitations (figures, supplementary files, PDF metadata each need separate handling), and the workflow is concrete enough that someone could follow it immediately.
