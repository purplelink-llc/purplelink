## Option 1

I launched Resume Review this week. It is the first Purplelink product not aimed at academic researchers.

The tool reuses the Paper Review pipeline almost entirely: upload a document, a panel of AI personas reads it in parallel from different angles, a consensus layer surfaces findings that more than one persona raised, you get a structured report. For Resume Review the three personas are an ATS Screener, a Hiring Manager Skeptic, and a Recruiter Red Flags pass. The price is $5.

While wiring it up I found a bug that had been sitting in the payment infrastructure since Paper Review went live. Modal, where the backend runs, was holding a test-mode Stripe secret key. Netlify, where the checkout lives, was using the live key. Invoice generation and the referral coupon feature were quietly minting against test-mode Stripe. The live and test dashboards have completely separate records, so nothing was obviously wrong in either place. I caught it only by verifying Resume Review's checkout end-to-end before launch.

The fix was straightforward: update the Modal secret to the live key and recreate the referral coupon in live mode. But finding it the hard way was a reminder that end-to-end payment testing in the actual production environment matters more than I had been giving it credit for.

If you have run into a test/live environment mismatch in a solo product, I would be curious how it surfaced.

## Option 2

Paper Review's pipeline starts by extracting references from an uploaded manuscript. For single-column PDFs this has worked cleanly. For two-column academic papers, I found this week it was silently failing: 4 of 5 real two-column papers I tested extracted zero references. Each failed for a different reason.

The first issue: pdfplumber's default x_tolerance of 3 is too coarse for tightly-kerned justified academic text. It merges adjacent words with no space between them, producing tokens the reference parser cannot match.

The second: pdfplumber's default reading order interleaves both columns onto the same output line for two-column pages. A sentence from the left column and a sentence from the right column appear concatenated. References that cross a column boundary fragment in ways that break any citation pattern.

The third: the "References" heading itself landed merged onto the tail of the previous line ("...unmod- References") or the front of the next line ("References DeSarbo, Wayne S.,..."), and numbered section headings like "9 References" never matched the original check at all.

I fixed each independently: lowered the tolerance, added a gutter-detection heuristic that extracts each column half separately with a fallback if the crop would lose content, and replaced the heading check with one that handles all three malformed shapes without false-positiving on a table-of-contents entry like "9 References 32."

Verified against 5 real papers. 512 tests passed, zero regressions.

If you have worked on academic PDF parsing, I would be curious which edge case cost you the most time.

## Option 3

I ran a market research pass this week on nine potential MicroSaaS ideas: a compliance alert tool, a class-action matching service, a salary benchmarking tool, domain arbitrage, grant matching, a podcast clipping product, an SEO auto-fix tool, an M&A due-diligence feed, and a legal-deadline calculator.

Eight of the nine were either saturated by well-funded incumbents or structurally not viable for a solo operator. The legal-deadline calculator was the clearest example of the second problem: it sounds like a software product but it actually requires a standing legal research operation and carries malpractice-level liability. Not something to build without a law practice behind it.

The ninth, a compliance-change alert tool for small businesses, was the standout. The data is public and cheap to access. There are real market comparables at a proven price point (TaxJar, NexusMonitor at $19-69 per month). The underserved wedge is specific: multi-state labor law for very small employers (5-50 employees), which today is only sold bundled inside expensive full HR platforms rather than as a standalone product.

I wrote the design spec and am building it under Purplelink LLC, not a new legal entity. The scope is all 50 states plus federal from day one, which is deliberately larger than a minimal-MVP approach. The reason is that "monitors 5 high-activity states" is a tool that immediately fails its first customer who has employees in a sixth state.

If you have evaluated a list of product ideas using a structured framework, I would be curious what criteria caused the most debates.

## Selected

Option 2 -- it is the most technically concrete and specific, explains a real quality problem and its fix with enough mechanical detail to be credible, and speaks directly to the academic researcher audience that uses the tool.
